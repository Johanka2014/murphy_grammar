Can, could and (be) able to

A: Using 'Can'

We use can to say that something is possible or allowed, or that somebody has the ability to do something. We use can + infinitive. The negative is can't.

We can see the lake from our hotel.

Can you speak any foreign languages?

I'm afraid I can't come to the party on Friday.

B: When to use (be) able to

(Be) able to is possible instead of 'can', but 'can' is more usual. However, 'can' only has present (can) and past (could) forms, so we must use (be) able to for other tenses or forms.

Present Perfect: I haven't been able to sleep recently.

Infinitive with 'must': Applicants must be able to speak two languages.

With other modals: Tom might be able to come tomorrow.

C: 'Could' for Past Ability

Could is the past of 'can'. We use it especially for general past ability, permission, and with verbs of perception like see, hear, smell, taste, feel, remember, understand.

Senses: As soon as I walked into the room, I could smell gas.

General Ability: My grandfather could speak five languages.

Permission: We were totally free. We could do what we wanted.

D: 'Could' vs 'was/were able to'

Use could for general ability. To say someone succeeded in a specific situation, use was/were able to or managed to.

General: Jack was an excellent tennis player. He could beat anybody.

Specific: Yesterday, Jack and Andy played a match. Jack managed to beat him.

Important Exception:

The negative, couldn't, can be used in ALL past situations (general and specific).

We looked for David everywhere, but we couldn't find him.
